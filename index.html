Satellite Images reveal which countries cheat on their economic statistics

“The temptation is great for government officials to manipulate economic statistics. Foremost among them is gross domestic product, the most commonly used measure of a country’s economy, which often serves as a proxy for a government’s financial stewardship. In a free society, groups like the civil service, media, businesses, and opposition leaders serve as a check on the impulse to flatter GDP figures. In autocratic regimes, there are fewer barriers.

Turns out, there is an easy way to tell if a country’s GDP is being artificially inflated. How easy? Just look at the night sky.

A large body of research shows that the brightness of a country’s night-time lights, as seen from satellites, is highly correlated with GDP growth. The more money people have, the more likely they are to have lights on at night. Businesses will also stay open later, resulting in even more light.

If autocrats are goosing GDP, then the reported growth in those countries should be higher than night-time light data would suggest. This is exactly what the University of Chicago political scientist Luis Martinez found. In a recently released working paper, Martinez finds that in countries with similar growth in night-time brightness, the more autocratic regimes report higher GDP growth. His analysis examined the World Bank’s GDP growth data, Freedom House’s political freedom ratings, and satellite data from the US’s National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration from 1992 to 2008.”

The ‘Best’ Trading Algorithms

This week, we’ll prime you for next week's Machine Learning Python Deep Dive with some simple machine learning models.

It remains that the easier to implement and understand the model is the better the algorithm is to use but if we look at algos that are successful at competition level neither a single decision tree nor linear regression ends up making the top of the ranks.

Successful models, from the research that I’ve done, fall into two main categories:

  • Tree based frameworks
  • Deep learning models
    • Tree Based Framework

    i. Concatenations of thousands of decision trees.

    What are decision trees?

    Decision trees are a set of rules used to classify data into categories. These types of algorithms tend to determine the most relevant data and the ‘tree’ is created by partitioning the data further. At each such point, we choose the question that allows for the bets split. Thus, we are ‘maximising information gain’. This process is recursive!

    A key advantage of a tree based framework against other models is that the results can be explained and justified; this is not always the case for neural networks for example. Often regulation dictates the model decisions must be explainable.

    ii. Gradient Boosted Trees.

    What are Gradient Boosted trees?

    This is a single tree that is recursively refined, until the weights given to each piece of data being fit to assist the algorithm in defining the fit. XGBoost is considered to be one of the best libraries to do this.

    What is XGBoost?

    It’s short for eXtreme gradient Boosting. This is a library designed and optimized for the boosted tree algorithm. Gradient Boosting is an approximation of Gradient Descent. Gradient Descent is a function that we aim to minimize.

    Deep Learning Based Framework

    What is deep learning?

    Deep Learning uses neural networks to learn the relationships. It is an area within machine learning that uses multiple layers of increasingly rich functionality. Classes of Deep Neural Networks use feed-forward networks that work with convolution and pooling layers. Deep-Learning techniques were able to beat traditional machine learning problems such as image classification and speech recognition.

    Algo Concept: Statistical Arbitrage

    Statistical Arbitrage is the buying and selling of many different securities with the aim of the portfolio manager, forecaster, to ‘neutralise’ risk-factors, based on statistically backed predictions.

    Fundamental to the concept is that, for example, the current price of a share is incorrect from the price that the forecaster believes it will return to.

    Forecasts are imperfect in the practical world and so there will be winning and losing trades. But the expectation is that the winning trade profits will exceed the losing trades.

    Statistical Arbitrage Algorithms are based on mean reversion theory, mostly as a pair. For example, if Apple’s price falls under $1 then Microsoft will fall by $0.5 but Microsoft has not fallen, so you will go and sell Microsoft to make a profit.

    Types of Statistical Arbitrage Trading Strategies

    Directional trading

    In directional trading, each instrument’s signal is independent of another instrument in the portfolio. Here, the individual price series is stationary.

    Pairs, Triplets and other co-integrated portfolio trading

    In pairs trading, signal is triggered based on the difference in relative value between two or more instruments.

    Index arbitrage

    In Index arbitrage, signal is triggered based on the difference in relative value between an index’s constituents and an index future.

    Algorithmic Trading Concept

    High-Frequency trading is... a type of algorithmic trading in which large volumes of shares are bought and sold automatically at very high speeds.

    It aims to increase liquidity (how easily something can be sold in the market) and makes market spreads (the difference between the bid and ask prices) tighter. An article to give you a scale of the HFT industry.
